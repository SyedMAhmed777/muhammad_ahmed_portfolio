01 — Overview

A pet you keep alive by keeping yourself alive.

Most quit-smoking apps are built like spreadsheets — counters, charts, "days clean" badges that feel either preachy or punishing. Nicotina takes the opposite stance: it's a game, with a tiny tomato (or cucumber, or mushroom) that you're keeping company on a journey out of nicotine.

The product blends habit-tracking, behavioural nudging and a virtual reward economy so that choosing not to smoke earns you something tangible — XP, gems, a new hat for your veggie, hours added to a "Life Won" counter, or a real-world cost comparison.

02 — The Problem

Quitting feels like punishment. It shouldn't.

Through interviews with people on (and off) quit attempts and a teardown of the leading apps, four patterns kept surfacing — each one a moment where motivation collapsed.

01

Streaks become anxiety

A 47-day streak is glorious — until day 48 turns it into a 0. Many users delete the app rather than face the reset.

02

Savings feel abstract

"$120 saved" doesn't motivate when it's just a number. People want to see what that money becomes.

03

Slip-ups feel like failure

Apps treat a relapse as the end of the world; behavioural research treats it as data. Most products don't reflect the science.

04

There's a void where the cigarette was

No one talks about the 3pm hand-to-mouth ritual. Without a replacement habit, willpower runs out around week two.

"Every app I tried felt like a doctor wagging a finger at me. I needed something that cheered when I did the right thing — not just guilt-tripped me when I didn't."

— User interview, 31, third quit attempt

03 — Goals

Four principles guided every screen.

Make progress feel earned, not measured

XP, levels and trophies — not just counters. Every healthy choice produces a small, visible reward.

Replace the cigarette with a habit, not a void

A daily Alternatives card suggests a small, concrete action — sugar-free gum, a 5-min walk, an apple — to fill the ritual gap.

Show the cost in human terms

Money saved isn't a number — it's a smartphone, a vacation, a year of Spotify. The Wealth Calculator makes the trade-off visceral.

Treat slip-ups as data, not shame

The diary asks "did you smoke?" and "how did you feel?" — never "did you fail?" Streaks measure check-ins, not perfection.

07 — Key Screens

Every screen, organized by what the user is trying to do.

29 screens across 10 feature areas.

7.1 — Welcome

A first impression that doesn't lecture.

Mascots and a hand-lettered logo set the tone in three seconds: this is going to be playful, low-stakes, and a little weird in the best way.

Splash

Hand-drawn logo + the two heroes say "this is a game" before any words do.

Get Started

Honest framing: "this app only works if you do 2 things." Sets expectations early — no false promises.

How to Play

A single page that explains the entire mechanic — red when you slip, blue when you replace.

7.2 — Authentication

Sign in, or skip the form altogether.

A "Continue as guest" path lowers the bar to entry — habit data first, account later. Error states stay friendly, never red-screen-of-death.

Sign in

Username/email + password, with "Continue as guest" right under the primary.

Sign up — empty

Four fields max. Social SSO at the bottom for the "I'll use Google" crowd.

Sign up — filled

CTA stays calmly disabled until validation passes. Inline helper text, no shouting.

Sign up — error

A worried cucumber softens the "Ooops!" — even the failure state is in-character.

7.3 — Personalization

Five questions, one avatar.

A short quiz tunes the entire experience: what you smoke, how often, your goal, your reminder cadence, and the veggie that'll keep you company.

What you smoke

Pill-style multi-choice + cost + nicotine content from a database lookup.

How often

Two stacked sliders + an honest "Not Sure" — no guilt for not knowing.

Your target

Stop · Decrease · Maintain. Three different games inside one app.

Choose your veggie

Three personalities to pick from — and you name them.

Reminder timing

Wheel-style time picker. "Never" is a valid answer — no dark patterns.

7.4 — Home Screen

Where the whole product earns its keep.

The home is the 30-second daily ritual: see your level, check the mood strip, press the big red or blue button, and move on with your day.

Level + XP at the top

Progress is the first thing you see — a soft, persistent reminder that you're moving forward.

Stars and gems, top right

Two currencies: stars for streaks, gems for milestones. Spend them in the shop without ever paying real money.

Mood strip — a single tap

😵 → 😟 → 😐 → 😊 fills the data the diary needs without making the user "journal".

The two buttons

Blue = "I replaced". Red = "I smoked". The whole behavioural model, reduced to two pixels of intent.

7.5 — Diary

Honest with yourself, one tap at a time.

A small, scrollable list of every day you logged — paired with a single feeling. No paragraph-writing required.

Diary

Each row tells a tiny story: "Smoked · I felt really bad" / "Didn't smoke · I felt superb."

Add entry

Calendar → Yes/No → 5 mood pills → optional note. Logging a day takes under 8 seconds.

7.6 — Progress & Reporting

Numbers that feel like a win.

Two surfaces: the bright-and-loud Levels view (XP, trophies, streak), and the quiet-and-deep Reporting view (calendar, money saved, mood trend).

Levels

Hexagons unlock left-to-right. Trophies sit in a quiet shelf below — present, but not nagging.

Reporting

Daily/Weekly/Monthly/Yearly tabs. Money saved + life regained sit right under the calendar.

7.7 — The Calculators

Make the cost visceral.

Two tools turn abstract numbers into something you can feel: time you'd win back, and the things that money becomes.

Hub

Two simple cards. No nested menus, no "advanced settings" hidden behind a gear.

Life Calculator

Slider-driven. Output: time won + money saved + nicotine avoided — three concrete numbers.

Wealth Calculator

A car. A vacation. A laptop. Pink cards = out of reach. Green = within reach.

7.8 — Habit Replacement

A new ritual, every day.

Behavioural science says quitting is easier when you replace, not just remove. A new card unlocks each day — small, doable, and finite.

One alternative per day, locked ahead

"Day 1: Sugar Free Gum" → "Day 2: Eat an Apple". The ? cards build curiosity without overwhelming.

Tied to the daily check-in

Marking "I replaced" on the home screen is what unlocks the next day's card — the loop closes.

Curated, not generated

Suggestions come from a vetted list of 90+ research-backed substitutes — not an LLM hallucination.

7.9 — Reward Economy

A reason to come back tomorrow.

Stars and gems earned through daily check-ins buy cosmetic items for your veggie. No pay-to-progress, ever — premium items are clearly labelled "Subscriber only".

Shop · Categories

Background, Wall, Pot, Table, Decoration — your veggie has a whole apartment.

Shop · Accessories

Glasses and hats. Tiny dopamine, repeatable forever.

Items · Buy

Star-priced and gem-priced items side-by-side. "Subscriber only" is honest, not sneaky.

Owned · Equip

A separate tab so the act of "wearing" stays distinct from buying.

7.10 — Account & Community

The boring screens, designed with the same care.

Profile, Settings, Spotlight (community events) and Notifications. Familiar patterns — every list row has the same warmth as the rest of the app.

Profile

Your veggie up top, then a calm list — Achievements, Notifications, Community, Stats…

Settings

Five rows. The dangerous one ("Delete All Data") gets a coral icon — earning attention without alarming.

Spotlight

Community events, IRL meetups, and seasonal campaigns ("Nicotine-free February").

Notifications

Two clear actions per row — Mark as Read · Delete. No nested swipe gestures.

08 — Outcomes

What changed once people started playing.

Tested with 9 participants in moderated sessions over two weeks — 4 social vapers, 5 long-term smokers. Tasks included finishing onboarding, logging a "smoked" day without rage-quitting, and using the Wealth Calculator to set a personal goal.

9/9

Completed personalization without skipping — the avatar reveal kept everyone hooked to the end.

0

Participants who deleted the app after their first "smoked" log — the diary's gentle framing held.

4.7/5

"Feels like a game I want to play" rating — vs. 2.4/5 for the leading competitor in the same test.

WHAT WORKED

  • · The Wealth Calculator was the most-screenshotted screen — users showed it to partners and friends, turning the app into a conversation.
  • · The two-button home (red/blue) made the daily action obvious. Average check-in time: 11 seconds.
  • · The mascot reveal at the end of personalization had the highest "delight" score in unprompted feedback.
  • · Coral was correctly read as "this is the bad-day option" without any explanation in 8/9 tests.

WHAT I'D CHANGE NEXT

  • · Build a real empty state for the home — first-time users see "0/0" stats and no XP, which feels lifeless.
  • · Add a "soft slip" pattern: when a user logs a smoke, the avatar should react with care, not just reset the streak to 0.
  • · Surface the Alternatives suggestion before the user reaches for the red button — pre-empting the craving.
  • · Explore a buddy system in Spotlight — pair quitters at similar stages so the social fabric isn't only one-way.
